Federal US Laws Against Stalking & Harassment

Our team can assist qualified individuals in organizing their case by preparing a clear chronological timeline of events and a comprehensive evidence catalog. A well-documented timeline helps investigators, attorneys, and courts understand what occurred, when it occurred, and how each event relates to the overall pattern of conduct. We can also help identify, organize, and preserve available evidence, including emails, text messages, call logs, screenshots, social media activity, account records, financial documents, photographs, videos, police reports, and other relevant materials. Properly cataloging evidence with dates, sources, and descriptions can make it easier for law enforcement, legal counsel, or the court to review the information efficiently. Our assistance is limited to organizing and documenting information. We do not determine whether any law has been violated, provide legal advice, or guarantee any particular outcome. Individuals should consult qualified legal counsel regarding their rights, potential claims, and the applicability of any federal or state laws to their specific circumstances.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 875(c) — Interstate Threats. Makes it a federal felony to transmit, in interstate or foreign commerce, any communication containing a true threat to injure another person. Applies to communications transmitted by telephone, email, text message, social media, or the Internet. Penalty: up to 5 years' imprisonment and a fine.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 1028 or 18 U.S.C. § 1028A — May apply if the perpetrator impersonates the victim or uses the victim's identifying information unlawfully while creating accounts or subscriptions.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 1030 (Computer Fraud and Abuse Act) — Prohibits unauthorized access to protected computers and online accounts, including conduct involving hacking, unauthorized access to government systems, or using compromised accounts to facilitate stalking, harassment, or other crimes.

  • 18 U.S.C. §§ 1030(a)(1)–(5) — May apply where unauthorized access is used to submit fraudulent online forms, place unwanted orders, create abusive subscriptions, interfere with computer systems, or obtain information without authorization.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 1343 — Wire Fraud. Prohibits schemes to defraud using interstate wire communications, including the Internet, email, or telephone.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 1029(a) — Access Device Fraud. Criminalizes fraud involving unauthorized access devices, account numbers, or authentication credentials affecting interstate or foreign commerce.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 2261A — Interstate Stalking (Violence Against Women Act, as amended). Makes it a federal felony to use interstate travel, mail, telephones, or electronic communications (including the Internet) to engage in stalking or harassment that causes substantial emotional distress or places a person in reasonable fear of death or serious bodily injury.

  • 47 U.S.C. § 223(a)(1)(C) — Harassing Telecommunications. Prohibits using telecommunications devices with intent to annoy, abuse, harass, or threaten another person under circumstances specified by the statute.

  • 47 U.S.C. § 227 — Regulates automated calls and text messages. While it primarily governs the entities sending robocalls or autodialed texts, it may become relevant if fraudulent enrollment causes unlawful automated communications to be sent to the victim.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 2425 — Use of Interstate Facilities to Entice a Minor. Makes it a federal crime to knowingly use the Internet, telephone, or other means of interstate commerce to communicate with a minor with intent to entice or solicit unlawful sexual activity.

  • Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA), including 18 U.S.C. §§ 2510–2523, and the Stored Communications Act (18 U.S.C. §§ 2701–2713) — May apply where electronic communications are unlawfully intercepted or where stored electronic communications or accounts are accessed without authorization.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 2511 — Prohibits the unlawful interception of wire, oral, or electronic communications, including certain unauthorized recordings of telephone calls or interception of emails while in transit.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 2701 and 18 U.S.C. § 1030 — May apply to unauthorized access to another person's email account, cloud account, or other protected computer systems.

  • 18 U.S.C. §§ 2511, 2512, and 1030 — May apply to the installation or use of spyware, keyloggers, or other interception or credential-stealing software, depending on the circumstances.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 2512 — Prohibits manufacturing, distributing, possessing, or advertising certain devices primarily designed for the surreptitious interception of electronic communications.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 2520 — Provides a civil cause of action for persons whose communications have been unlawfully intercepted in violation of the Electronic Communications Privacy Act.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 2707 — Provides a civil cause of action for violations of the Stored Communications Act involving unauthorized access to stored electronic communications.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 3771 — Crime Victims' Rights Act. Provides victims in federal criminal proceedings with rights including notice of proceedings, the opportunity to be heard, and treatment with fairness, dignity, and respect.

  • 18 U.S.C. § 2265 — Full Faith and Credit for Protection Orders. Requires states, tribes, and U.S. territories to recognize and enforce qualifying protection orders issued by other jurisdictions.

  • TAKE IT DOWN Act (Public Law 119-12) — Establishes federal criminal penalties and takedown procedures for the knowing publication or threatened publication of nonconsensual intimate visual depictions, including qualifying AI-generated intimate images, as provided by the Act.

Oregon US Laws Against Stalking & Harassment

The following Oregon statutes may apply to stalking, harassment, identity theft, unauthorized computer access, electronic communications offenses, and related conduct, depending on the specific facts of a case. State criminal investigations are generally handled by local law enforcement agencies, district attorneys, and, where appropriate, the Oregon Department of Justice. In addition to criminal prosecution, some conduct may also support civil remedies, including protective orders or civil lawsuits under Oregon law.

  • ORS 163.732 — Stalking. Prohibits repeated and unwanted contact that causes a person reasonable apprehension regarding their personal safety or the safety of an immediate family or household member. Stalking is generally a Class A misdemeanor but may become a Class C felony under specified aggravating circumstances.

  • ORS 30.866 — Civil Stalking Protective Order. Authorizes eligible victims to petition the court for a stalking protective order and provides a civil remedy independent of any criminal prosecution.

  • ORS 166.065 — Harassment. Prohibits specified forms of harassment, including offensive physical contact and conduct intended to harass, annoy, or alarm another person under circumstances defined by Oregon law.

  • ORS 164.377 — Computer Crime. Prohibits unauthorized access to computers, computer systems, networks, or data, including conduct involving fraud, theft, damage to computer systems, or unauthorized use of another person's computer resources. Depending on the conduct, violations may constitute a misdemeanor or a felony.

  • ORS 165.800 — Identity Theft. Makes it a Class C felony to knowingly obtain, possess, create, transfer, or use another person's personal identifying information with intent to deceive or defraud.

  • ORS 163.720 — Unlawful Disclosure of Private Information. Prohibits knowingly disclosing another person's personal information with the intent to stalk, injure, or damage the person or the person's property under circumstances specified by the statute.

  • ORS 163.715 — Unlawful Use of a Global Positioning System Device. Restricts the unauthorized use of GPS tracking devices to monitor another person or vehicle in circumstances prohibited by Oregon law. Certain violations may constitute a felony.

  • ORS 646A.600–646A.628 — Oregon Consumer Information Protection Act. Requires businesses to safeguard personal information and provide notice of qualifying security breaches involving personal information. These provisions may become relevant where personal information has been compromised.

North Carolina Laws Against Stalking & Harassment

The following North Carolina statutes may apply to stalking, harassment, identity theft, unauthorized computer access, electronic communications offenses, and related conduct, depending on the specific facts of a case. Criminal investigations are generally handled by local law enforcement agencies, district attorneys, and, where appropriate, the North Carolina State Bureau of Investigation. Depending on the circumstances, victims may also have access to protective orders and civil remedies under North Carolina law.

  • N.C. Gen. Stat. § 14-277.3A — Stalking. Prohibits willfully engaging in a course of conduct directed at a specific person, without legal purpose, that would cause a reasonable person to fear for their safety or the safety of immediate family or close associates, or to suffer substantial emotional distress. Depending on the circumstances and prior convictions, stalking may be prosecuted as a misdemeanor or felony.

  • N.C. Gen. Stat. § 14-196 — Harassing Telephone Calls. Prohibits making telephone calls with the intent to abuse, annoy, threaten, harass, or embarrass another person under circumstances prohibited by the statute.

  • N.C. Gen. Stat. § 14-458 et seq. — Computer-Related Crime. Prohibits unauthorized access to computers, computer systems, computer networks, or electronic data, including hacking, computer fraud, theft of information, disruption of computer services, and other unlawful computer-related conduct.

  • N.C. Gen. Stat. § 14-113.20 et seq. — Identity Theft. Makes it a criminal offense to knowingly obtain, possess, or use another person's identifying information without authorization for unlawful purposes, including financial fraud, impersonation, or obtaining anything of value.

  • N.C. Gen. Stat. § 14-190.5A — Disclosure of Private Images. Prohibits the intentional disclosure of another person's private images without consent under circumstances specified by North Carolina law.

  • N.C. Gen. Stat. Chapter 50C — Civil No-Contact Orders. Authorizes victims of unlawful conduct, including stalking or nonconsensual sexual conduct by persons with whom they do not have a qualifying personal relationship, to petition the court for a Civil No-Contact Order.

  • N.C. Gen. Stat. Chapter 50B — Domestic Violence Protective Orders. Provides eligible victims in qualifying personal relationships with the ability to seek a Domestic Violence Protective Order (DVPO), commonly known as a "50B Order."

Pennsylvania Laws Against Stalking & Harassment

The following Pennsylvania statutes may apply to stalking, harassment, identity theft, unauthorized computer access, electronic communications offenses, and related conduct, depending on the specific facts of a case. State criminal investigations are generally handled by local law enforcement agencies, county district attorneys, and, where appropriate, the Pennsylvania Office of Attorney General. In addition to criminal prosecution, some conduct may also support civil remedies, including protection orders or civil lawsuits under Pennsylvania law.

  • 18 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 2709.1 — Stalking. Prohibits engaging in a course of conduct or repeatedly communicating to another person under circumstances that demonstrate an intent to place the person in reasonable fear of bodily injury or cause substantial emotional distress. Depending on the circumstances and prior convictions, stalking may be charged as a misdemeanor or felony.

  • 18 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 2709 — Harassment. Prohibits conduct intended to harass, annoy, or alarm another person, including repeated communications, following a person in public places, or engaging in other specified harassing behavior.

  • 18 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 7611 et seq. — Unlawful Use of a Computer and Computer Crimes. Prohibits unauthorized access to computers, computer systems, computer networks, and electronic data, including conduct involving fraud, theft, disruption of services, or unauthorized use of another person's accounts or information.

  • 18 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 4120 — Identity Theft. Makes it a criminal offense to possess, transfer, or use another person's identifying information without consent to further an unlawful purpose. Penalties increase based on the value involved and the number of victims.

  • 18 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 4954 — Intimidation of Victims or Witnesses. Prohibits intimidation, retaliation, or attempts to interfere with victims or witnesses involved in criminal investigations or judicial proceedings.

  • 23 Pa. Cons. Stat. Chapter 61 — Protection From Abuse (PFA). Provides eligible victims of domestic violence, stalking, or related abuse with the ability to petition the court for a Protection From Abuse Order under circumstances authorized by Pennsylvania law.

  • 18 Pa. Cons. Stat. § 2718 — Unlawful Dissemination of an Intimate Image. Prohibits the unlawful dissemination of intimate images without consent under circumstances specified by Pennsylvania law and may provide criminal penalties for qualifying conduct.

Texas Laws Against Stalking & Harassment

The following Texas statutes may apply to stalking, harassment, identity theft, unauthorized computer access, electronic communications offenses, and related conduct, depending on the specific facts of a case. State criminal investigations are generally handled by local law enforcement agencies, county and district attorneys, and, where appropriate, the Texas Department of Public Safety. In addition to criminal prosecution, some conduct may also support civil remedies, including protective orders or civil lawsuits under Texas law.

  • Texas Penal Code § 42.072 — Stalking. Prohibits engaging in a course of conduct directed at another person that the actor knows or reasonably should know the other person will regard as threatening bodily injury or death, the commission of an offense against property, or conduct that causes the victim or a member of the victim's family or household to fear bodily injury, death, or property damage, or to feel harassed, annoyed, alarmed, abused, tormented, embarrassed, or offended. Stalking is generally a third-degree felony, with enhanced penalties for repeat offenders.

  • Texas Penal Code § 42.07 — Harassment. Prohibits certain communications and conduct intended to harass, annoy, alarm, abuse, torment, or embarrass another person, including repeated electronic communications, telephone calls, and threats under circumstances specified by the statute.

  • Texas Penal Code § 33.02 — Breach of Computer Security. Prohibits knowingly accessing a computer, computer network, computer system, or electronic device without the effective consent of the owner. Enhanced penalties may apply depending on the nature of the offense.

  • Texas Penal Code § 32.51 — Fraudulent Use or Possession of Identifying Information. Makes it a felony to obtain, possess, transfer, or use another person's identifying information without consent and with intent to harm or defraud. Penalties increase based on the number of identifying items involved.

  • Texas Penal Code § 21.16 — Unlawful Disclosure or Promotion of Intimate Visual Material. Prohibits the disclosure or promotion of intimate visual material without the depicted person's effective consent under circumstances specified by Texas law.

  • Texas Code of Criminal Procedure, Chapter 7A — Protective Orders. Authorizes courts to issue protective orders for victims of stalking, trafficking, sexual assault, abuse, or other qualifying offenses, regardless of whether criminal charges have been filed in certain circumstances.

  • Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code Chapter 98B — Liability for Intimate Visual Material. Provides a civil cause of action for certain victims whose intimate visual material has been unlawfully disclosed, allowing recovery of damages and other relief authorized by law.

Arizona Laws Against Stalking & Harassment

The following Arizona statutes may apply to stalking, harassment, identity theft, unauthorized computer access, electronic communications offenses, and related conduct, depending on the specific facts of a case. State criminal investigations are generally handled by local law enforcement agencies, county attorneys, and, where appropriate, the Arizona Attorney General's Office. In addition to criminal prosecution, some conduct may also support civil remedies, including protective orders or civil lawsuits under Arizona law.

  • A.R.S. § 13-2923 — Stalking. Prohibits intentionally or knowingly engaging in a course of conduct directed toward another person that causes the victim to reasonably fear for their safety, the safety of an immediate family member, or to suffer emotional distress under circumstances specified by Arizona law. Depending on the conduct involved, stalking may be prosecuted as a felony.

  • A.R.S. § 13-2921 — Harassment. Prohibits knowingly engaging in conduct directed at a specific person that seriously alarms, annoys, or harasses the person and serves no legitimate purpose, including repeated unwanted contact or communications under circumstances prohibited by the statute.

  • A.R.S. § 13-2316 — Computer Tampering. Prohibits knowingly accessing, altering, damaging, disrupting, or obtaining information from a computer, computer system, computer network, or other digital device without authorization. Depending on the circumstances, violations may constitute felony offenses.

  • A.R.S. § 13-2008 — Taking the Identity of Another Person or Entity. Makes it a criminal offense to knowingly use another person's or entity's identifying information without authorization for an unlawful purpose, including fraud, financial gain, or obtaining a benefit.

  • A.R.S. § 13-1425 — Unlawful Disclosure of Explicit Images. Prohibits knowingly disclosing or threatening to disclose explicit images of another person without consent under circumstances specified by Arizona law.

  • A.R.S. §§ 13-3602 and 12-1809 — Orders of Protection and Injunctions Against Harassment. Authorize Arizona courts to issue protective orders for qualifying victims of domestic violence and Injunctions Against Harassment for victims who do not have a qualifying domestic relationship with the respondent.

  • A.R.S. § 13-2916 — Use of an Electronic Communication to Terrify, Intimidate, Threaten, or Harass. Prohibits using electronic communications to terrify, intimidate, threaten, or harass another person under circumstances specified by Arizona law.

Mindfulness Therapy Does Not Work For Everyone

Mindfulness-based therapies have become a prominent part of modern mental health care through the convergence of Buddhist contemplative practices, medical research, and evidence-based psychology. As mindfulness was incorporated into approaches such as "DBT" and "trauma-informed care", it became increasingly common in clinical practice. As a result, some patients find it difficult to access alternative therapeutic approaches. While no single therapy works for everyone, treatments such as traditional Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) are well-established options, particularly for individuals seeking alternatives to mindfulness-based techniques. We recommend working with a licensed mental health professional while we conduct our investigation. Whether you are experiencing trauma, coordinated harassment, or both, a clinician can provide appropriate care while documenting the psychological impact when relevant.


Mindfulness can be effective for managing mild to moderate stress, anxiety, depression, and chronic pain, but it is not a cure-all. It is not a primary treatment for PTSD, psychotic disorders, OCD, or ongoing, reality-based threats such as digital stalking or harassment. For some individuals, mindfulness may increase distress, making alternatives such as traditional Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) or trauma-focused therapies a better fit.
